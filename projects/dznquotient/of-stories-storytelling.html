Of stories & storytelling

‘We are all stories in the end, just make it a good one eh?’ -Stephen Moffat

Stories have since time immemorial been used as means of influence. It’s an extremely persuasive instrument. It evokes emotions & sentiments and can sweep you off your feet. It’s a world of magic made real by words, and in our digital world made real visually.

Storytelling is an art, and good designers always have a good story to tell. How important is it really? We tell you- very much so!

It’s the very skeleton that shapes your brand perception. Ideally it should be incorporated right from the beginning and used in Experience design and in the Design Thinking process. Storytelling builds an enthralling plot and helps designers develop a deep and emotional understanding of their Clients & the Consumer’s motivations and needs. They are fantastic tools to infuse empathy into your design project, and can be extremely useful for design thinkers.

Pompous storytelling is often more opposing than convincing, while statistics are often get glossed over. Artistic merging of rhetoric and facts, woven into the right story, can alter minds. Here we’ve listed some rudiments of good storytelling.

Involve your audience…...Interaction is the key

There is nothing more boring than a presenter who wants to get done with the presentation without sighting the response or signal from his audience. If you are dancing in a dark room then it doesn’t matter what people think of your performance but if you are telling a story, there are minds waiting for something to happen.

Be present to your audiences’ non-verbal signals and body-language. Try to keep it interactive by asking questions or make them think of the subsidiary context. Rather than telling them ‘2+2=?’, You ask what do you think ‘? + ? = 4’ There is a clear difference between two points made here, former makes your audience think of only one answer but the latter allows them to be more than just right. It allows them to be actively involved in the subject.

Predictable is boring! Be Mercurial

Don’t restrict yourself to PPT’s pointers or slides full of data to push your point forward. An abstract piece of art can convey or evoke same emotion with minimal data as well. Fill in the blanks…..…. Once upon a time there was ______ every day, ______ one day______ Because of that, _____ Until Finally_______... You can practically fill in the blanks with anything you want to highlight in a storytelling session and make it worth sharing.

Don’t shy away from using physical objects to present an idea. Any quixotic twist in the flow of your presentation will actually refresh your audience and they wouldn’t feel the urge for a breather. Don’t limit your idea to a screen, instead use the physical space to make it tangible instantly.

Trigger Emotions… by having one.

Giving precedence to ‘values’ over ‘attributes’ of the product or idea, can be really beneficial. Bringing out compassion in the way you sell a product works wonders.

‘Why’s’ and ‘How’s’ will follow suit if you have a good ‘What’. Find the difference between these two short stories below:
Once upon a time, there was a king and a queen. Both Died!
Once upon a time, there was a king and a queen. King died and Queen died of grief!
Let them visualise their own version.

You wonder why when you see a movie based on a book that you’ve read before; you usually feel disappointed? A book allows us to imagine a character based on few words, it accentuates curiosity and creates an imaginary world. You interpret and deduce your conclusion; you imagine the character and you create the scenes. A film is someone else’s interpretation of the story, it cuts short imagination and intrigue.

So, what do you do? You co-create by involving stakeholders to form a common idea or solution. They take pride in owning and creating it & you get to ‘make it happen’. Unleash the potential of your audience to create something bigger and personal.

Short & Sweet

Most adults know when to stop selling (we emphasize on the most). Try selling an idea to a kid. If you can get hold of the kid’s attention throughout your conversation then chances are high that it will be an interesting presentation. Kids are full of questions & the level of their involvement can be your plunger with regards to the quality of your storytelling.

Just ask yourself how a 7-year-old would respond to you. There is a kid residing in all of us which takes charge of our emotions and expectations more often than not. Cater to that kid while doing some serious grown-up business.

Talk the talk

Be there as a medium through which the story is told & be a good one. Please don’t let the mental rubbish of facts or past presentations deviate you from creating a connection with your audience.

Remember - What we know is also what limits us. See it as an opportunity to become better at what you do rather than proving it to the audience. Every attempt to impress the audience makes the presentation weaker than the intent of serving with relevant information.

Takeaways

If you want to create wow moments, simultaneously driving your point across and entertaining, then add some recallers at the end. Finish your presentations with happy endings while subtly retelling your message to get it to register in audience’s head. If possible, give them something they can take away with them like a ‘point of view’ or ‘possibility’.

 

In the end, we could say without a good story, many ideas dwindle and fade, brands go unnoticed, products fail to make a mark, and visions get buried. A good branding / product or campaign needs a solid story behind it, so it can be comprehended and reminisced. So, create your plot, throw in the characters, add diction, songs and conflicts and of course don’t forget the grand climax!
